# Voice and style rules

Distilled from the Google developer documentation style guide, the Microsoft Writing Style Guide, and plain-language guidance. Most are enforced by slop-cop.

## Person and mood

- Address the reader as you and your. Never use we, our, or us for the reader. Reserve first-person plural for the documenting project, with a clear antecedent.
- Write procedure steps in the imperative; the you is implied, as in "Run the command." and "Create the file."

## Voice and tense

- Use active voice and make the actor the grammatical subject, as in "The server sends an acknowledgment." Allow passive only to emphasize an object or de-emphasize an irrelevant actor.
- Use present tense for general behavior. Ban future "will" and hypothetical "would" except for time-specific events.

## Sentences and structure

- One idea per sentence. Keep the average around 20 words and under 26. Put subject, verb, and object near the start.
- Front-load every level. Lead the page, section, and paragraph with the most important point, and put the topic sentence first.
- Start statements with a verb. Edit out "you can", "there is", and "there are".
- Put conditions before instructions. Write "If X, do Y", not "Do Y if X".
- Keep paragraphs to three to seven lines. Use numbered lists for sequences and bulleted lists otherwise. Use parallel structure for parallel items.

## Words

- Prefer short, common words over long ones. Write "start", not "commence". Define any unavoidable jargon on first use, and expand acronyms on first use.
- Use exactly one term per concept everywhere, with identical capitalization. Keep a project term map and never introduce a synonym for the same thing.
- Cut condescending fillers in procedures, such as "simply", "just", "easy", and "quickly". Cut placeholder phrases, such as "please note" and "at this time".
- Use contractions for warmth. Ban exclamation marks, slang, internet slang, and pop-culture references.

## Headings and punctuation

- Use sentence case for titles and headings. Omit end punctuation on headings and short list items.
- Use the Oxford comma. Use standard American spelling.

## Inclusive language

- Use gender-neutral terms. No generic he or she; rewrite to you, a plural, or a role, and use singular they when needed. Never "he/she" or "s/he".
- Focus on people, not disabilities. Do not write "suffering from".
- Replace biased technical terms, such as primary and subordinate instead of master and slave, or allowlist and blocklist. Leave literal or quoted uses alone.

## Code in text

- Use backticks for filenames, paths, commands, function and class names, keywords, types, and placeholder variables. Do not code-font product names or browsable URLs.
- Do not inflect a code element name. Add a noun and inflect the noun, as in "the `Event` flag", not "`Event`s".
